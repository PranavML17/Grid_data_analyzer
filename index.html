BESCOM (Karnataka) connection process
5 steps to energise an EV charging hub. Verified June 2026. bescom.karnataka.gov.in
1
Site feasibility check
Visit BESCOM sub-division. Request load availability at nearest 11kV feeder for your required kVA. This tells you if a dedicated transformer is needed and how loaded the feeder is.
Timeline: 1–3 days
2
Submit LT-6 / HT-2 application
Apply at bescom.karnataka.gov.in → New Service Connection. Choose LT-6(c) for public DC fast charging. HT-2(f) if your aggregate load exceeds 100 kW.
Timeline: 7–15 working days

3
BESCOM demand note + payment
BESCOM issues a demand note: 3-month security deposit + service line charges + DT contribution if a new transformer is needed at your feeder.
New DT cost: Rs 4–12 lakh if required
4
CEA electrical safety compliance
CEA Installation Regulations 2006 apply. A BESCOM-licensed wiring supervisor is required. Get an electrical test certificate before energisation.
Wiring certificateElectrical test reportBIS-certified charger invoice
5
ToD meter install + energisation
BESCOM installs a Time-of-Day meter. No separate electricity distribution licence is needed to sell electricity for EV charging (MoP Sep 2024 order).
End-to-end: 6–10 weeks no new DT · 3–5 months if new DT needed

📖 Key terms explained
Grid & electricity terms
Grid headroom MOST IMPORTANT
How much spare electricity capacity the local substation has for new connections. If a substation is running at 90% of its maximum capacity, there is very little "headroom" — connecting a new high-power charger will be slow, expensive, or refused. At 55% loaded, there is plenty of headroom — new connections get processed quickly and cheaply.
Example: Whitefield 220kV substation = 57% loaded → HIGH headroom → new 120kW charger connection: 4–6 weeks. Koramangala 66kV = 93% loaded → LOW headroom → new connection: 4–6 months + augmentation required.
Loading %
What percentage of a substation's maximum power capacity is currently being drawn. 60% loading = comfortable, room for new connections. Above 85% = constrained zone, new large connections face delays. Above 93% = effectively full, new HT connections are typically blocked.
Think of it like a motorway: 60% capacity = traffic flows freely. 90% = slowdowns. 100% = gridlock, no new vehicles can join.
DISCOM
Distribution Company — the electricity utility that operates the wires and meters in your area. They set your tariff, process new connection applications, and approve or reject your load sanction. Your DISCOM is the first call for any EV charging or BESS project.
Bengaluru: BESCOM. Mumbai: MSEDCL. Chennai: TANGEDCO. Hyderabad: TSSPDCL. Delhi: BSES / TPDDL.
HT vs LT connection
LT = Low Tension (below 1kV, typically up to ~100 kW). HT = High Tension (11kV or 33kV, for loads above 100 kW). Large EV charging hubs typically need an HT connection. HT connections take longer to approve, require a dedicated meter and substation, but have lower per-unit tariff.
Demand charge
A fixed monthly fee on your electricity bill based on your peak power demand in kW — separate from the energy (kWh) you consume. For large commercial and industrial users, demand charges can be 30–50% of the total electricity bill. BESS can reduce it by preventing demand spikes.
If your factory peaks at 500 kW for just 15 minutes in a month, you pay the demand charge on 500 kW for the whole month.
Round-trip efficiency (RTE)
The fraction of energy you put into a battery that you can get back out. 90% RTE means for every 100 kWh stored, you recover 90 kWh — the other 10 kWh is lost as heat. Higher RTE = lower effective cost per kWh cycled through the battery.
LFP cells: 92–96% RTE. NMC cells: 88–93% RTE. A 2% RTE difference on a 10 MWh battery over 1,500 cycles = ~300 MWh of energy lost.
ToD pricing (Time of Day)
An electricity tariff where the per-unit price changes based on time of day. Off-peak (10pm–6am) rates are significantly lower than peak (6pm–10pm) rates. Critical for BESS arbitrage: charge cheaply at night, discharge (or displace peak consumption) during expensive hours.
EV charging terms
Utilisation %
For a charging station: the percentage of total available charging time when a vehicle is actually plugged in and drawing power. 100% would mean the station is always fully occupied — impossible in practice. India's public stations averaged 15–35% utilisation in 2025. Higher utilisation = faster payback.
A 4-gun, 18-hour station at 25% utilisation is effectively delivering the same energy as a 1-gun station running 18 hours at 100%.
Breakeven utilisation
The minimum utilisation % at which the station covers all its costs (energy + lease + opex + maintenance). Below this level, every hour costs money. Above this level, every hour generates profit. A critical sanity check before committing to a site.
Charge point vs station
A "station" is the physical installation. A "charge point" (or "gun") is each individual connector that can serve one vehicle at a time. A station can have 2, 4, or 8+ charge points. The calculator uses charge points as the unit of capacity.
